Access control is being rewritten for agents that write their own code.

A reviewer used to be a bottleneck. That was the point: nothing shipped faster than a person could reasonably think about it. Once the thing proposing changes stops being a person, the bottleneck disappears, and most of what access control assumed for fifty years disappears with it.

A world built for named accounts and quarterly reviews

Picture a typical onboarding: a new engineer gets a laptop, a set of credentials scoped to what their role needs, and a seat in the review queue. Someone, eventually, checks that the scope still makes sense. That is least privilege in practice, and it has worked reasonably well for decades because the requester was always a person with a manager, a start date, and a limited number of hours in the day. Code review is the same story wearing different clothes: one engineer proposes something, another reads it, and the whole system runs at the speed of two people thinking. It is a good design. It was simply never designed for a requester with no start date and no limit on hours.

What breaks first is not security, it is arithmetic

An agent can open a dozen reasonable changes before your first coffee finishes. Each one, read on its own, looks fine. A reviewer who is used to three or four changes a day is now facing forty, and something has to give. It is rarely a dramatic failure. The review still happens, a checkbox still gets ticked, but the attention behind it thins out until "reviewed" stops meaning what it used to mean. The old model does not break with an alarm. It breaks by continuing to look exactly like it is working.

Separation of duties, applied one level deeper

The fix is not a new idea. Keeping the person who starts a transaction separate from the person who approves it is old enough to predate computers, standard in banking and long-required in flight software. What is new is pushing that same separation inside the change itself: one identity proposes, a second and genuinely distinct identity checks, and neither is ever allowed to be the same actor wearing two hats. Ideally that second identity is built differently enough from the first that it does not carry the same blind spots. The judgment that used to belong to one trusted person is now split across roles that check each other, because nobody, human or not, gets to grade its own homework anymore.

A grant that expires instead of a grant that lingers

Cloud security already fought this exact battle and the answer it settled on was just-in-time access: a credential exists only for the window it is actually needed, granted on purpose rather than left switched on because nobody remembered to switch it off. The same pattern is showing up wherever teams give agents write access. Autonomy becomes a deliberate setting, scoped narrowly to what has actually earned trust, just as easy to revoke as it was to grant. A short list of paths, the pipeline configuration, the secrets, the schema, stays behind a human gate no matter how much trust the rest of the system has earned, because the cost of getting those specific things wrong never gets smaller.

Building the habit, not writing the policy

Teams that get this right are not the ones with the longest policy document. They are the ones that turned a handful of decisions into habits: distinct identities for proposing, checking, and merging before any write access is granted at all; a short, explicit, memorized list of paths that always need a person; a pass or fail decision that lives somewhere the change under review cannot touch; and a switch for broader autonomy that an owner turns on for a reason, and can turn back off just as fast. None of that is exotic. It is the same discipline good teams already applied to people, extended one more step to cover a new kind of contributor.
